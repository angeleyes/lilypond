Hi Han-Wen,

Here's my personal knowledge on basso continuo numbering (I'll give you the historical definition if you desire, it'd be an easy task, but I figured you'd prefer getting down to the brut interesting facts for Lilypond).

Principal for numbering bass are the following (this was the most common method):

  • The perfect chord (is that correct in English, its a chord like <c e g> I think [usually 'perfect triad' in English-raybro]) when unaltered doesn't have any numbering.
  • Each number corresponds to an interval beetween a note from the resulting "chord" (nobody actually does brut chords) and the continuo (non-stop stream of notes). Eg: 3 = third, 4 = fourth, etc.

To simplify things, (this is more for people creating bass figures), complementary notes do not get numbered Eg: sixth chord is number 6 (and not 6|3), the seventh chord is numbered 7 (and not 7|5|3)...

[to unsimplify things, for people trying to read or notate them: In more usual terminology, root chords (eg, c e g, stacking a major fifth over a major third) receive no numbers, the written note indicating the root and the major third and fifth being assumed; first inversion chords (e g c, stacking a major sixth over a major third) are notated with only a 6 under the notated root; second inversion chords (g c e, stacking a sixth over a fourth) receive a stack of 4 under 6, lined up under the note that indicates the root, and third inversion (which infers a seventh added to the triad which is in the root location, eg, Bb c e g) is almost never used in common baroque practice. If it were, it could be 'spelled' with 2 4, under the notation for the root-position note (the seventh of the chord). In baroque practice, 7 under a note means to add the seventh to the top of the major chord built on the notated root. A "sixth chord" would be notated with a stack of 3/5/6 (if the fifth were to be included)It is otherwise identical to the first inversion. Numbers are stacked by value, lowest to highest. Additionally, the above examples (on the major chord ceg) presumes a major key: in a minor key (indicated by the key signature) c eb g would be indicated by no numbers, and the numbers would indicate scale degrees, not necessarily major intervals. -raybro]

  • An accidental next to a number affects the interval represented by that number. When alone or below a number, it affect the third.

[Because the figured bass notations are separate from the staff, it is not uncommon to indicate minor chords with a flat, even in keys where the third's notename would require a natural to make a minor chord. Although this sometimes leads to confusion, it is rare in period sources. -raybro]

  • A dashed number means the interval is modified (this shouldn't be replaced by a sharp or a flat, because it won't act as so. Dashed numbered must be added).
  • Some composers imagined placing a cross (used in the numbering as would be an accidental), to indicate a sensible note.

[note for these last two: Dashes are sometimes used (especially in theoretical texts) to indicate that the previous harmony is kept, even though the bass has changed. Dashes and pluses may be used to indicate diminished or augmented intervals. Sometimes crosses ranging from plus-sign appearance to highly stylized dagger appearance are used to indicate ornamentation. I have found no indication that period composers (or modern-ish theorists) find this wide ambiguity to be any bother at all. -raybro]

I hope this is all very clear to you, though I doubt it.. [editorial additions clearly marked with brackets, spelling corrections without indications and apologies to the originator, whose original text wasn't bad or incorrect, but seemed to deserve a little more explanation. -raybro]
